Teaching philosophy

School should leave students more dangerous in a good way

I do not want students leaving class just knowing a few commands. I want them leaving class more able to think through confusion without folding the second something gets hard.

Coding is useful because it gives immediate feedback. The computer does exactly what you told it, not what you meant. That makes it a strong place to practice precision, patience, and better thinking.

I also care about the deeper layer under the code. Identity. Assumptions. Certainty. What counts as knowing something. How to hold a belief without becoming trapped inside it. Those questions matter to me, so they show up in the classroom too.

Why I teach

I like helping people understand things that used to feel out of reach

I was not the kid floating through school effortlessly. That ended up helping me. It forced me to get good at explaining ideas in a way that actually lands.

A lot of school trains students to chase grades, comply, and move on. I hated that. I still do. The version of teaching I care about feels different. More alive. More honest. More built around real understanding.

One of the earliest clues came when I taught tennis at a Nike camp as a teenager. I liked the feeling of helping someone get better at something in a way they could feel immediately.

How I run class

Try. Fail. Notice. Adjust. Try again.

That loop is at the center of a lot of what I do. Video games understand this better than school often does. Students stay engaged when they can act, get feedback, revise, and see progress.

I want collaboration in the room. I want students explaining things to each other. I want them to see that being useful to other people matters. Sometimes the lesson is in the code. Sometimes the lesson is in what kind of person other people want around them.

  • Hands on work over long lectures
  • One clear concept at a time
  • Projects that feel like something, not filler
  • Room for discussion, ideas, and real questions

What I’m building

I care about tools that help students create, not just consume

Right now a lot of my work sits at the intersection of teaching, AI, entrepreneurship, and better digital experiences for students.

I’m interested in building class experiences that are cleaner, smarter, more interactive, and more honest about what actually helps students learn.

I want my students to leave with more than coursework. I want them to leave with proof that they can make things, solve problems, and build a life they respect.

There is no speed limit to learning. The real danger is deciding too early what kind of person you are and then living inside that sentence.

A belief that shapes how I teach and how I keep learning.
