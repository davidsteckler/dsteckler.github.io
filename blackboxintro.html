BLACK BOX: START

One Lucky Break

Before you begin the scavenger hunt, look at the message below. Right now, it may look like nonsense. That is the point. Most hard things start as a black box.

Bill Gates got rare access.

When Bill Gates was a student at Lakeside School, he got access to a computer terminal at a time when almost no high school students had that chance. That did not make him successful by itself, but it gave him a door most people never got to open.

Morgan Housel calls this luck.

In The Psychology of Money, Morgan Housel uses Gates as an example of rare luck. Gates had a one-in-a-million opening. His friend Kent Evans faced the opposite force: one-in-a-million risk.

LUCK AND RISK

Luck and risk are both forces outside your control. One can open a door. One can close a door. So the lesson is not “successful people just worked harder.” The better lesson is this: when a lucky door opens, you still have to be the kind of person who can walk through it.

This scavenger hunt is your lucky door today. You are being handed a strange message, a few clues, and time to figure it out. The question is not whether it feels confusing at first. It will.

The real question is whether you can stay with the confusion long enough for it to turn into thought.

Start when you are ready to decode the first message.
The light bulb comes after staying with the hard part.
