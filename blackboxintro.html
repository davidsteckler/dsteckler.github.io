Chapter 2: One lucky break

Bill Gates did not begin with Microsoft.

He began with access.

In 1968, Lakeside School had something almost no school had: a computer terminal connected to a time-sharing computer. Gates was in eighth grade. He was thirteen. Most teenagers in the world never got near a computer. He did.

That was luck.

But luck was not the whole story.

A lucky break is only an opening. Gates used the opening. He stayed with the machine. He learned the language. He wrote programs. He and his friends spent strange hours trying to understand what this new tool could do.

The computer did not hand him a future.

It gave him a black box.

He looked inside.

That is why this scavenger hunt starts the way it does. You are given something confusing. A block of binary. A program. A sandbox. No full explanation at first. You have to slow down, use the tool, and make the hidden message appear.

Most lucky breaks do not look exciting when they arrive.

They look like a weird assignment. A tool you do not understand yet. A class you did not know would matter. A problem that makes no sense until you stay with it longer than most people would.

Gates once said that without Lakeside, there would have been no Microsoft.

That does not mean effort does not matter. It means effort needs something to work on. Sometimes the door is luck. Sometimes the work is noticing the door while it is still just a black box.

Pay attention. Use the tools. Keep going.
