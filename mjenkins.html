Dr. Merv Jenkins · Renaissance Learning

See every student.

A talk about reluctant learners, music, vocabulary, reading shame, adult humility, and the danger of confusing protection with defiance.

Literacy Vocabulary Student voice School to prison pipeline Music as memory
The point

Some students are not refusing school. They are surviving it.

That was the throughline. What looks like attitude may be shame. What looks like defiance may be fear. What looks like laziness may be a student trying not to be exposed.

The adult who sees first

His mother applied to colleges for him before he believed college was even part of his story. That is what it means to see potential before a student can name it.

Music opens the door

People remember songs, chants, prayers, rhymes, and rhythms. Music makes language stick because it carries emotion, timing, and identity.

Reading shame hides

A student who cannot read may clown, argue, skip, or shut down because those moves feel safer than being found out.

The stories

Four moments that carried the talk.

01

The bus ride

Everyone was naming colleges. He had not applied. So he said he was going to New York City to become a rapper. His friends could see it. His mother saw something bigger.

02

The envelopes

South Carolina State. Benedict College. Two acceptances he did not create for himself. His mother had already moved on his behalf.

03

The walkie talkie

As an assistant principal, he almost called the resource officer on a student who cursed instead of reading aloud. Then he realized the student probably could not read.

04

Macbeth as a beat

Ms. Nehemiah saw him rapping and gave him Shakespeare. She did not reject his rhythm. She redirected it into academic power.

The move is not “lower expectations.” The move is to notice the student clearly enough to build the bridge.

“Someone saw ___ in me, and they helped me ___.”

Why that prompt worked

It made every adult in the room remember being shaped by another person’s attention. Creativity. Intelligence. Music. Space. Transformation. Those answers were not about programs. They were about being recognized.

Literacy

Vocabulary is access.

The reading activity made adults feel what students feel: confusion, hesitation, and the slow panic of being expected to perform with only partial understanding.

75 percent comprehension

Even adults struggled. That was the point. If 75 percent comprehension feels uncomfortable, imagine students trying to move through school at 50 percent or below.

Background knowledge matters

A math or science person may see meaning in a passage others miss. A hip hop person may know “cipher” in a way others do not. Reading is vocabulary plus context plus prior knowledge.

A student’s behavior may be the visible symptom of an invisible literacy problem.

What to remember

Care has to become instruction.

See the whole student

Do not reduce the student to the behavior in front of you. Ask what the behavior is protecting.

Come prepared

The student voice in the lyrics was clear: love can look like preparation, scope and sequence, standards, tutoring, and clear instruction.

Use data humanely

Assessment should not be a label machine. It should help adults see gaps early enough to do something useful.

Open polished session notes
Dr. Merv Jenkins opened with a story about being a reluctant learner. On the bus ride home, he heard classmates calling out where they were going next: Clemson, South Carolina State, Morris College, Atlanta, Morehouse. He had not applied anywhere. He slid down in his seat until someone asked where he was going. Under pressure, he said he was going to New York City to become a rapper. His friends believed him. His mother did not. When he told her, she challenged him. He argued that if he had applied to college and gotten accepted, they would not be having the argument. Then she reached behind her back and handed him two envelopes. South Carolina State had accepted him. Benedict College had accepted him. He had not applied. His mother had applied for him. That became the first big idea of the talk: young people need adults in front of them who see what they cannot yet see for themselves. He then asked the room to remember the first song, poem, rhyme, chant, or prayer they ever memorized. People answered instantly. The point was simple and powerful: music, rhythm, and words stick. They open memory. They invite participation. He named himself as the reluctant learner. School did not excite him. Lunch did, because lunch meant beatboxing, rhyming, and feeling alive. He connected that to students who appear disengaged, disruptive, or defiant. Sometimes the behavior is not the real story. Sometimes it is protection. He told a story from his time as an assistant principal at Chapel Hill High School. He was leading a freshman handbook activity and asked a ninth grade student to read aloud. The student stared at him, then cursed at him. Jenkins reached for the walkie talkie to call the school resource officer, then stopped. Something told him the student could not read. He put the walkie down, called on another student, and kept the room moving. He later explained what might have happened if he had made the other choice. The resource officer might have arrived. There might have been physical engagement. The student might have ended up on the news as a kid who fought a police officer and disobeyed an administrator. The news would not have said the real cause: a student who could not read had been exposed in front of his peers. When Jenkins became a middle school principal, one of his hiring questions was: What puts a student at risk? The answer he valued most was: sometimes adults put students at risk. Not always intentionally. But still. He called that humility. He moved into lyrics written as a student speaking to a teacher: “I wish you could see me.” The student was not asking for pity. The student was asking for empathy, preparation, literacy support, math support, and a teacher who shows love by coming prepared. The teacher voice answered: “I see you. I used to be you.” The talk then returned to his own life. In high school, Ms. Nehemiah saw him rapping and performing every day. Instead of dismissing that, she connected it to Macbeth. He still remembered the rhythm and language years later. The lesson: good teaching does not erase what students already love. It translates that energy into academic power. He asked the audience to fill in this reflection: Someone saw ___ in me, and they helped me ___. Responses included creativity, intelligence, space, transformation, and music. The room was not just talking about school. It was talking about being noticed. Then he shifted into vocabulary and comprehension. Vocabulary is not a side issue. It shapes access. It shapes confidence. It shapes behavior. It shapes a student’s odds of feeling like school belongs to them. He ran a reading activity at 75 percent comprehension. Most adults struggled. Then he pointed out the reality: many struggling readers are operating at 50 percent or below. Imagine walking into every class except lunch and needing to read when reading itself feels like failure. He connected this back to behavior. A student who cannot read may become the class clown, provoke the teacher, get removed, skip school, or shut down. That may look like laziness or defiance from the outside. Inside, it may be shame management. He also explained the role of background knowledge. A person with math or science background may understand a passage that others cannot. A person with hip hop background may understand a word like “cipher” differently. Comprehension is not just decoding. It is vocabulary plus context plus prior knowledge. He closed by previewing vocabulary work with hip hop and classroom music, pointing toward reading comprehension and vocabulary acquisition as game changers. He also introduced 9th Wonder and their shared history, including the way a connection led into major music opportunities. The thread held: words can move people. Words can save people. Words can also expose people when we are careless.
