Things I've Learned
Using AI Tools

After a lot of trial and error with ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Claude, and Perplexity — here's what actually makes a difference.

01 Be specific. Vague questions get vague answers. +

The more precise your question, the more useful the response. Generic prompts produce generic output — narrow it down before you hit send.

Instead of
"Tell me about programming."
Try
"Explain the basics of Python programming for a 7th-grade student."
02 Give it context. The AI doesn't know who you are. +

A little background information dramatically changes the quality of responses. Tell it your level, your purpose, or your situation.

Instead of
"Explain variables."
Try
"As a beginner in computer science, explain what variables are in programming."
03 Break big tasks into smaller ones. +

Asking "teach me to code" is like asking someone to teach you to cook — it's not a single question. Slice it into concrete, answerable pieces.

Instead of
"Teach me how to code."
Try
"What programming languages are easiest to learn for a complete beginner?"
Then
"Give me a simple beginner project to practice basic programming concepts."
04 Use examples to show what you mean. +

Concrete examples remove ambiguity — both in what you're asking for and in what you get back.

Instead of
"Explain loops."
Try
"Explain how 'for loops' work in Python, using a real-world example."
05 When the answer is confusing, just ask. +

There's no embarrassment here — if the response uses a term you don't understand, ask it to clarify. That's the whole point of the conversation.

AI says
"Functions allow you to encapsulate code."
You follow up
"What does 'encapsulate' mean in this context? Give me a simple example."
06 Tell it how you want the answer formatted. +

You're in control of the output format. Want a list? A table? A short paragraph? Just say so.

Instead of
"Tell me how to study effectively."
Try
"Give me five study tips in a bulleted list, each with a one-sentence explanation."
07 Ask it to show its work on hard problems. +

For anything involving logic, math, or reasoning — asking for step-by-step explanations both improves accuracy and helps you actually understand the answer.

Instead of
"Solve this: 3x + 5 = 20."
Try
"Solve 3x + 5 = 20 and explain each step so I can follow the logic."
08 Its knowledge has a cutoff — recent events are a blind spot. +

AI models don't have access to today's news. For anything recent, either use a tool with web access (like Perplexity) or be explicit about the timeframe.

Instead of
"What are the latest AI developments?"
Try
"What were the major AI developments up through your knowledge cutoff? Flag anything that may have changed since."
09 Give it a role to play — the response quality shifts. +

When you frame the AI as an expert or a specific kind of person, the tone, depth, and relevance of responses tends to improve. It's a small trick with a real effect.

Instead of
"Give me study advice."
Try
"As an experienced math teacher, what study strategies would you recommend for a student who keeps making careless mistakes?"
10 State your constraints upfront. +

Budget, time, skill level, available materials — the more limitations you name, the more useful the suggestions will be.

Instead of
"Suggest some science projects."
Try
"Suggest five computer science project ideas for a 7th grader with no budget and only a school laptop."
11 Ask for both sides, not just one. +

By default, the AI will often give you the most agreeable or popular take. Push it to consider the other side — it makes the output more useful and honest.

Instead of
"Explain the importance of homework."
Try
"What are the strongest arguments both for and against homework in middle school? Don't lean either way."
12 Ask for sources — but verify them yourself. +

Asking for citations is a good habit, but AI tools sometimes invent references that sound plausible. Always double-check anything important before relying on it.

Instead of
"What are the benefits of coding for students?"
Try
"What are the benefits of learning to code for students? Cite any studies or sources you're drawing from."
13 Keep the conversation going — follow-ups work. +

You don't have to fit everything into one prompt. AI handles back-and-forth well — treat it like a conversation, not a search box.

First ask
"What is a function in programming?"
Then follow up
"Can you walk me through a real example of that function in Python, with comments?"
14 If the answer is off, try rewording the question. +

Sometimes the AI just doesn't understand what you're really after. Rather than repeating yourself, rephrase — a different angle often unlocks a much better response.

Instead of
"Why should I learn coding?"
Try
"What are the practical benefits of learning to code for a middle school student who's interested in sports and games?"
15 AI has biases baked in — worth keeping in mind. +

These models are trained on human-generated text, which means they carry the biases of that data. On anything nuanced or contested, actively prompt for balance.

Instead of
"Are video games bad for students?"
Try
"What do researchers say about how video games affect students — positive and negative? Include findings from both camps."
16 Request specific formatting when it matters. +

If you're copying output into a document or presentation, asking for a specific format saves a lot of cleanup. You can even specify markdown, HTML, or plain text.

Example
"List the benefits of learning programming as bullet points. Bold the key term in each one. Keep each point to one sentence."
17 Adjust the tone — it'll match whatever style you need. +

Formal, casual, funny, blunt — the AI will adapt its voice to whatever you describe. Getting the tone right makes responses far more usable.

Example
"Explain algebra as if you're a middle schooler who loves basketball and hates math class. Keep it real, not corny."
18 Use it as an idea machine — ask for a long list. +

One of AI's genuine superpowers is volume. Ask for 20 or 30 ideas and you'll almost always find a few genuinely useful ones you wouldn't have thought of yourself.

Example
"Give me 25 project ideas for a 7th-grade computer science class. Include a mix of easy, medium, and ambitious ones."
19 Get advice through a specific mentor's lens. +

Asking "what would a seasoned entrepreneur say about this?" produces a different response than asking for generic advice — and usually a more useful one.

Example
"As a mentor who's been brutally honest with students their whole career, make a real case for why I should work hard in school. Skip the platitudes."
20 Use it for things that are hard to Google. +

Search engines are great for finding information that exists somewhere. AI is better at synthesizing, reasoning, and tackling messy problems that don't have a clean answer page.

Example
"My code is supposed to print the numbers 1 to 10 but it only prints 1. Here it is: [paste code]. What's wrong and why?"
The better you communicate, the better the output. These aren't rules — they're patterns worth building into the habit.
