David Steckler  /  AI Best Practices

Prompting
Tier List

Not all prompting habits are equal. These are ranked by how much they actually change the quality of your results — starting with the ones that matter most.

S Must-know. These alone will double your results.
A High-impact habits worth building early.
B Solid techniques once you have the basics.
C Situational. Useful for specific use cases.
D Nice to know, rarely changes the outcome.
S
Essential
The foundations. Ignore these and everything else is a waste of time.
01 Be specific. Vague questions get vague answers. ▼

This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do. The model is trying to find the most likely useful response to your words — every ambiguous word is a guess it has to make on your behalf. Nail down who you are, what you want, and what format you expect, and almost everything else gets better automatically.

Instead of
"Tell me about marketing."
Try
"I run a small online pottery shop. What are three low-cost marketing channels that work well for handmade goods, and why?"
02 Give it context about yourself and your goal. ▼

The AI has no idea who you are. It doesn't know if you're a student, a doctor, or a professional with twenty years of experience. Without that, it defaults to some imaginary average person — which is probably not you. A single sentence of background reorients the entire response.

Try opening with
"I'm a high school teacher with no coding background. I want to understand enough about APIs to explain them to students in plain English..."
03 Treat it like a conversation, not a search box. ▼

Most people write one prompt, get one answer, and move on. That's the least effective way to use it. Follow up. Push back. Ask it to expand on one part and simplify another. The second and third responses are almost always better than the first — and the model remembers everything said earlier in the thread.

After any response, try
"That third point is interesting — go deeper on that one specifically."
04 Push back when the answer is wrong or weak. ▼

AI models are inclined to be agreeable, which means they'll sometimes confidently give you a mediocre answer rather than admit uncertainty. If something feels off — say so, and explain why. Don't just say "try again." Tell it what was wrong. "That answer assumed I had a budget — I don't. Try again with that constraint." This is one of the fastest ways to dramatically improve output quality.

One caveat: the model can also be too easily swayed in the other direction. If you push back and it caves completely without explanation, ask it to defend its original answer — that'll tell you whether it was wrong or just being agreeable.

05 Ask for step-by-step reasoning on anything complex. ▼

When you ask the model to think through a problem step by step, it actually produces better answers — not just for your benefit. Forcing it to reason through steps reduces errors in math, logic, and analysis. This is called chain-of-thought prompting and there's real research behind it.

Add to any complex question
"...think through this step by step before giving me your answer."
A
High Impact
Worth building into your habits early. You'll notice the difference immediately.
06 Give it a role to play — it changes the response quality. ▼

Framing the AI as an expert in a specific domain shifts the tone, depth, and vocabulary of the response. It also helps the model decide which assumptions to make about your needs. "As a senior software engineer" produces different output than "As a computer science teacher for 7th graders" — even for the exact same question.

Example
"As a no-nonsense personal finance advisor, give me honest feedback on this budget: [paste budget]."
07 Break big tasks into smaller steps. ▼

One massive prompt asking for everything at once usually produces a bloated, shallow response. Decompose the problem — first ask what approach to take, then go deeper into each piece. You'll get more useful output for the same total effort, and it's easier to redirect when one part goes wrong.

08 Ask it to critique your own work. ▼

One of the most underused prompts is simply: "What's wrong with this?" Paste in an email, a plan, a piece of code, an argument — and ask the AI to find the weaknesses, gaps, or errors. You'll often get feedback that's more useful than anything you'd get from a generic "improve this" request.

Try
"Read this email draft and tell me what's unclear, what might land badly, and what I've probably left out: [paste draft]"
09 State your constraints upfront. ▼

Time, budget, skill level, tools available, audience — the more constraints you name, the more practical the output. Without them, the model tends to produce idealized solutions that assume unlimited resources. The real world has constraints; tell it what yours are.

Instead of
"How do I grow my newsletter?"
Try
"I have 200 subscribers, post twice a month, and have no marketing budget. What are realistic ways to grow to 1,000?"
10 Start a new conversation when things go sideways. ▼

AI models carry the full context of a conversation forward — which is usually great, but becomes a liability when earlier exchanges were confused or went off the rails. If the model is stuck in a weird pattern, producing poor responses, or has been corrected so many times it seems disoriented, don't keep fighting it. Start fresh. Copy your best prompt and try again clean.

B
Solid Technique
Good habits once the fundamentals are down. Situationally very useful.
11 Ask it to rephrase or simplify when confused. ▼

If an explanation isn't clicking, don't reread it hoping it'll click — ask for a different angle. "Explain that like I'm 12" or "Give me an analogy" or "Can you use a concrete example?" almost always helps more than rereading the same abstract explanation.

12 Specify the output format you want. ▼

Want a table? A numbered list? A short paragraph, not an essay? Say so. The default format is often longer and more padded than necessary. Specifying format also reduces the cleanup work after you get the answer.

Example
"Give me the answer as a two-column table: left column is the action, right column is why it matters. No intro text, just the table."
13 Use it as a brainstorming engine — ask for volume. ▼

Generating a lot of ideas fast is genuinely one of AI's strongest suits. Ask for 20 or 30 ideas on any topic and you'll almost always find a handful that are worth keeping — ones you wouldn't have reached on your own. Don't filter while generating; ask for quantity first, then refine.

Example
"Give me 30 newsletter topic ideas for a freelance illustrator. Be creative, include some unusual angles, and don't repeat yourself."
14 Rephrase the question when the answer is off. ▼

Sometimes the model just didn't understand what you actually meant. Rather than repeating yourself or typing the same prompt harder, try coming at it from a completely different angle. Change the framing, add a concrete example of what you're looking for, or strip it down to the core question.

15 Ask for the opposing view, not just confirmation. ▼

The AI will naturally produce responses that feel balanced but often lean toward the popular or expected view. For anything important — decisions, arguments, analysis — explicitly ask it to steelman the opposite position. "Now argue against the advice you just gave me" is a surprisingly useful follow-up.

16 Adjust the tone to fit where the output will be used. ▼

The default AI writing voice is often a bit too formal and polished to sound like a real person. If you're writing something that needs to sound human — a casual email, a social post, a Slack message — ask it to dial down the corporate sheen. Describe the audience and the context.

Example
"Rewrite this in a warmer, more direct tone. Cut the filler phrases. It should sound like it came from a real person, not a template."
C
Situational
Useful in specific contexts. Won't move the needle for everyday use.
17 Use the mentor framing to get blunter advice. ▼

Asking the AI to respond "as a mentor who won't sugarcoat things" tends to produce feedback that's more actionable and less hedged than default responses. Useful for evaluating ideas, getting career advice, or when you specifically want someone to tell you what's wrong with your plan.

18 Ask for sources — but verify them yourself. ▼

Requesting citations is a reasonable habit, but AI models sometimes fabricate references that sound credible. Treat any cited study or article as unconfirmed until you've checked it yourself. This isn't a reason to skip asking — it's just a reason to always double-check before you repeat something in your own work.

19 Use it for things that are hard to Google. ▼

Search engines are good at finding information that exists somewhere as a clean document. AI is better at synthesizing, reasoning through messy situations, and answering questions that are too specific or too contextual for search. The classic example: debugging code where the error depends on your specific logic, not a known bug.

20 Know that its knowledge has a cutoff date. ▼

For anything time-sensitive — recent events, current prices, who currently holds a position — the AI may be working with outdated information and won't always flag this proactively. If recency matters, use a tool with web access (like Perplexity or Claude with web search enabled) or verify the answer elsewhere.

D
Minor Gains
Fine to know. Rarely changes the outcome in practice.
21 Use examples to illustrate your request. ▼

Showing the AI an example of what you want — a sample email, a formatting style, a tone of voice — is genuinely useful when the output needs to match something specific. In practice, for most everyday prompts, being specific (Tier S) achieves the same thing. This becomes more important for creative work or when you need output to match an existing style exactly.

22 Ask for bold, bullets, or tables when it helps readability. ▼

Formatting requests are useful for cleanup, but they don't change the substance of an answer. "Bold the key terms" or "put this in a table" is useful mainly when you're copying output directly into another document. In most conversational contexts, the information matters more than the formatting.

23 Be aware of potential bias in AI responses. ▼

Worth keeping in mind, but this is more of a general epistemic habit than a prompting technique. The practical version of this advice is already captured elsewhere: ask for both sides, ask for counterarguments, and verify anything important before relying on it. "Be aware of bias" on its own doesn't give you a concrete thing to do differently.
