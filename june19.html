The Magic of Spontaneity: A Juneteenth to Remember

Yesterday, I had the day off for Juneteenth, and it turned into a memorable adventure. The original plan was simple: walk Pixie, our dog, hit the gym, and relax. But when we got to Lifetime, it was chaos—everyone was off, and every chair was taken. The pool was out of the question.

That's when Leigh’s gym friend mentioned her partner’s boat rental club. He gets a boat for half a day every two weeks in the summer. We decided to be spontaneous and join them at Stamford Harbor near PRIME. We quickly walked and fed Pixie (wishing she could join us) and headed out.

As soon as we set sail, the world seemed to fade away. The wind blew through my hair, the rhythmic sound of water against the hull made it easy to be fully present. We navigated from Stamford to Great Captains Island, seeing familiar sights like Tod’s Point Beach, Greenwich Avenue, and the Delamar Hotel from a new perspective.

Docking was a challenge. There were no mooring spots available. After some humorous attempts to anchor (I had no idea how to do it), we finally snagged a mooring spot when a boat left. Darren tied the knot—I should have asked him what type it was; I love learning new things.

On that hot 90-degree day, the water was an irresistible escape. We jumped in, feeling the mix of cold and warm spots, joking about how someone must have peed to make it warmer. Leigh, ever the adventurer, swam to another boat and asked if she could jump from their fourth floor. They agreed, and as she climbed, I swam nervously, waiting below.

Leigh made the jump, and I captured it on camera. As she surfaced, a man with a cigar clapped. It was cute to see her swim back, feeling proud of her daring leap. We wrapped her in a towel, and she recounted the thrill of the jump, her eyes sparkling with excitement.

However, at one point, I started to struggle in the water. The currents were stronger than I anticipated, and treading water for 20 minutes wiped me out. It was a stark reminder of how powerful the ocean can be and the importance of knowing your limits.

Back on the boat, we dried off and shared some of our favorite books from the past year. I talked about Derek Sivers’ "How to Live," a profound exploration of life philosophies. Darren shared "Killing Lincoln," a gripping account of Lincoln's assassination. Karen introduced "The Mountain Is You," about self-sabotage and personal growth. Leigh, not much of a reader, enjoyed listening and adding her thoughts.

Seeing a boat with dogs made me think of Pixie. Though she needs more practice in the car, the idea of her on a boat is exciting. This experience underscored the freedom boats offer—a break from routine and access to new adventures.

A key takeaway from this adventure is the importance of embracing spontaneity. Sometimes, the best experiences come from unplanned moments. An actionable tip: always be open to changing your plans, especially when an exciting opportunity presents itself.

Reflecting on this Juneteenth, I realize the power of spontaneity. Writing this at 10:38 PM, with my wife and dog asleep beside me, I’m grateful. The day ended with a simple meal from CVS and an episode of Stranger Things. Yet, it was perfect.

There’s no such thing as a bad day on the water. This experience reminded me of the importance of creating lasting memories and embracing the unexpected. Every moment on the water felt magical, reinforcing the lesson that sometimes the best days are the unplanned ones.
